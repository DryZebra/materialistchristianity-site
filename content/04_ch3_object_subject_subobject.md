# **Chapter 3: Object, Subject, Sub-Object** {#chapter-3:-object,-subject,-sub-object}

So far, we’ve treated reality as motion. What matters isn't what exists independently, but what causes change, especially change that persists, repeats, or reshapes behavior.

To describe this reality, we need categories that reflect the structure of motion. Historically, most descriptions fall into two groups: objects and subjects.

An object is a thing. It has mass, location, and inertia. It can be measured, moved, or broken. Objects exist independently of awareness. A stone, a building, a machine part, all are objects. They can affect us, but they don't decide.

A subject is a person. Or more precisely, it's a consciousness that interprets, chooses, or intends. A subject can reflect, believe, resist. Subjects don’t simply move, they act.

In most frameworks, these two categories are considered exhaustive. Everything is either a thing, or a being who perceives things.

But this division does not explain how most of human reality operates.

It does not explain institutions, which are not people, but aren’t objects either.  
It does not explain laws, which change behavior but are not conscious.  
It does not explain money, which has no agency, yet causes action.  
It does not explain rituals, names, or rules, all of which persist without a body or mind.

These structures are not objects. But they are not subjects.  
They exist between people. They persist through behavior.  
And they become real only through motion sustained across time.

We need a third category because some structures are not found in any one object, nor can they be reduced to the intentions of a subject. They exist between people, stabilized through repeated behavior, enforced through consequence, and carried through time. These are social realities, but they are also material.

Consider money, not as a coin or a bill, but as a function. Marx made clear that money isn't value itself. It's the form that value takes in circulation. Its power does not come from the material it’s made of, but from the social labor embedded in the system that accepts it. It condenses the past labor of others and commands future labor from others. That makes it real.

This is the commodity form: an object whose value does not arise from its use, but from its place in a social process. And when that process becomes regular, predictable, enforced, repeated, the form becomes stable. Money gains reality through motion, not essence.

Laws behave similarly. A law has no consciousness. it's not a subject. But it changes how people act. It has force, not because of its words, but because of the structures, courts, customs, police, reputations, that carry it. Its power is in its consequence.

So do rituals. A sacred practice might begin in shared intention, but over time, it continues through repetition. Its meaning adapts. But the motion persists. The candle, the meal, the silence, these objects help coordinate behavior. But the structure lives in the motion itself.

These structures are not beliefs. They are not physical objects. But they endure because they generate repeated, measurable motion.

And because they shape labor, trust, fear, and response, they are material.

They are not subjects. They are not objects.  
They are something else.

The patterns we’ve described, rituals, laws, monetary systems, traditions, are not objects, and they are not subjects. They belong to a third category: the sub-object.

A sub-object is a structure that exists between people, not in any one person or thing. It emerges from behavior, stabilizes through repetition, and becomes real through consequence. It does not have consciousness, but it causes change. It does not have mass, but it shapes action.

Sub-objects are not invented. They form through interaction. When enough people act in a way that aligns, repeats, and generates consequence, a sub-object begins to take shape.

It may begin informally: a pattern of trust, a shared rule, an expected gesture. Over time, if the motion continues, if it becomes legible, enforceable, and self-reinforcing, it solidifies. The structure persists, even as individuals come and go.

Unlike an object, a sub-object cannot be picked up, moved, or stored.  
Unlike a subject, it does not think, speak, or choose.  
But like both, it participates in reality, because it produces real effects.

Its primary material is human behavior, and its medium is social motion.

This isn't an abstract category. It has rules, conditions, and limits. Sub-objects are

* Relational (they exist between people)

* Patterned (they rely on repetition)

* Consequential (they produce real-world effects)

* And durable (they persist across time, even without constant awareness)

Sub-objects are not mystical. They are motion captured into structure.

Once formed, a sub-object does not rely on constant agreement or conscious belief. It persists through recognition and behavioral compliance.

Recognition does not require that every person explicitly identifies the sub-object. it's enough that they adjust to it. A workplace hierarchy, for example, does not need to be explained to every employee. it's felt, through tone, language, silence, and decision-making. The structure is known, even if it's never named.

This is because sub-objects apply pressure. They are not just patterns. They are enforced expectations. The enforcement may come from institutions, but more often it's distributed across many people: habits, glances, corrections, omissions.

The individual does not carry the sub-object alone. It exists across relationships. But it becomes real for the individual when it shapes behavior, when it creates hesitation, compliance, or fear.

This makes sub-objects extremely stable. They don't depend on individual conviction. They are enforced by the structure of consequence.

A person may disagree with the expectations placed on them, but still follow them. A worker may resist the logic of the clock, but still punch in. A student may question the value of grades, but still perform for the exam.

That tension, between private judgment and public compliance, isn't a failure of the sub-object. It's evidence of its strength.

What holds the sub-object in place isn't belief.  
its motion sustained under pressure.

Sub-objects don't begin with intention. They emerge from repeated behavior under specific conditions.

A new rule does not become a sub-object the moment someone writes it. It becomes one when people adjust to it. The adjustment may be slow, unconscious, or inconsistent. But once it stabilizes, once it becomes expected and enforced, it forms a structure.

This typically requires repetition across time, consistency across relationships, and consequence for deviation. A parent might correct a child’s behavior in a specific way. If the correction is echoed in school, reinforced by peers, and repeated in media, it becomes difficult to ignore. The behavior now carries a pattern, and that pattern begins to form a sub-object.

The process can be informal. A workplace might slowly develop an unspoken rule: don’t question the manager in meetings. If enough people follow it, and the consequences for breaking it are consistent, silence, exclusion, retaliation, the rule stabilizes. It begins to exist beyond any one person.

Over time, the behavior becomes less about strategy and more about structure. People follow the rule not because they are choosing to, but because that is the shape of the environment. The sub-object has taken hold.

Not all habits become sub-objects.  
Only those that

* Are repeated

* Are reinforced across relationships

* Are connected to consequence

* And persist beyond any single subject’s intention

Once those conditions are met, the motion becomes form.

And the form begins to act back on those within it.

Sub-objects appear in every part of life. Some are formal, like laws. Others are informal, like customs. Some are deliberate. Most are not.

A dress code, for example, isn't just a rule about clothing. It's a structure that regulates speech, access, and perceived legitimacy. The rule may be written, but the real enforcement occurs through tone, feedback, and opportunity. A worker out of uniform may not be punished directly, but they are often excluded, ignored, or passed over. The structure functions whether it's named or not.

Religious rituals follow a similar pattern. A moment of silence, a shared prayer, a seasonal fast, these acts create a pattern of behavior across people. The individual may feel personal meaning, but the ritual gains its force from repetition and recognition. It organizes motion. It sustains memory.

Classroom behavior is often shaped by sub-objects. A student may know the syllabus, but they also learn which questions are safe to ask, when to speak, and when to remain silent. These rules are not typically taught directly. They are absorbed. And once absorbed, they become hard to break, even when conditions change.

Language itself is structured by sub-objects. A phrase may lose its literal meaning, but continue to shape behavior through tone and context. Phrases like “man up,” “be professional,” or “respect the process” carry pressure. They frame expectations. They exist not in the words, but in the response those words create.

Each of these examples reveals the same pattern: motion becomes structure, and structure becomes pressure. The sub-object isn't an idea. It's organized consequence.

Revolutions produce sub-objects as quickly as they destroy them. A new flag, a motto, a declaration, these are not just symbols. They are tools meant to organize behavior, hold memory, and structure the new social order.

The American Revolution generated several sub-objects: the Constitution, the Bill of Rights, the Presidential oath, and the phrase “We the People.” These structures were not accidental. They were created to contain motion, to direct collective behavior beyond the moment of revolt. Thomas Paine recognized this clearly. In Rights of Man, he wrote:

“A constitution isn't the act of a government, but of a people constituting a government…”

This statement reflects more than a political opinion. It reveals a process: the transition from spontaneous action to sustained structure. The people’s motion, their strikes, refusals, assemblies, had to become legible, repeatable, enforceable. The Constitution functioned as a sub-object: a structure born from motion, now shaping motion in return.

Other revolutions followed similar patterns. The French motto, “liberté, égalité, fraternité”, was repeated so often it became a behavior. Citizens acted as though those values were present even when they weren’t. The repetition became enforcement. The phrase became a structure.

Over time, these revolutionary sub-objects outlasted the conditions that created them. Some became inert. Others were absorbed into institutions that used them for control. But in every case, the sub-object remained, still shaping behavior, still producing consequence.

This is the power of the sub-object: it doesn’t ask for belief. It asks for repetition. It survives not in the mind, but in the body, in the motions that people perform without questioning why.

Sub-objects don't last forever. Like any structure, they degrade when their internal motion weakens or their external conditions shift.

A sub-object decays when it no longer organizes behavior. This does not always happen suddenly. It may begin with contradiction, when people perform the structure, but no longer trust it. When the consequences begin to feel arbitrary. When the repetition no longer produces alignment.

In the United States, voting is one such structure. For much of the 20th century, the act of voting functioned as a sub-object: a stabilizing ritual that reinforced national unity and legitimacy. But as party lines hardened, outcomes felt predetermined, and systemic barriers persisted, participation shifted. People began showing up less. Others voted without belief. The ritual remained, but the motion fractured.

The “American Dream” functioned similarly. For decades, it aligned behavior: work hard, buy a house, support a family. But rising costs, stagnant wages, and systemic inequality disrupted that motion. People continued to speak the phrase, but fewer acted as though it were possible. The structure became symbolic, no longer integrated into real material conditions.

In some cases, it inverted. The phrase “living the dream”, once aspirational, became a form of irony. it's now often spoken in frustration, sarcasm, or quiet defeat. The motion remained, but the meaning reversed. This inversion reflects a deeper dialectical pattern: when a sub-object’s internal motion contradicts its conditions, the behavior persists, but now functions as critique.

Even post-9/11 patriotism showed signs of this pattern. Initially, it generated unified motion: flags, slogans, enlistment, sacrifice. But over time, contradictions emerged, wars without end, misinformation, surveillance. By the 2020s, gestures like “support the troops” persisted, but they no longer commanded the same behavioral unity. The sub-object had lost its stabilizing function.

These cases reflect a deeper process. Over time, people adapt in small ways. They show up late. They stop correcting violations. They mimic compliance without commitment. These adjustments appear minor, but they indicate a deeper shift: the sub-object is losing coherence.

In some cases, the structure is maintained by force long after it has decayed. A government continues to recite a founding principle that no longer resonates. A church recites rituals that no longer move its members. The form survives, but the motion has dispersed.

This kind of survival isn't stability. it's inertia without direction. The sub-object becomes hollow. It still applies pressure, but the pressure is no longer grounded in shared motion. it's no longer integrated. it's imposed.

At this stage, the structure is vulnerable. It may fracture under stress, collapse from within, or be absorbed into something else. What remains is often a symbol, referenced, defended, but no longer alive.

A dead sub-object isn't invisible. It remains as habit, nostalgia, or ideology. But its real function, its ability to organize behavior, is gone.

And once that function disappears, so does its place in the structure of the real.

Rupture does not stop motion. It only removes the structure that once held it. What follows isn't silence. It's reorganization.

When a sub-object collapses, the behaviors it once coordinated don't vanish. People still eat, work, speak, and coordinate. But without structure, these motions become fragmented. Improvisation takes the place of tradition. Strategy replaces trust.

In these moments, a new structure becomes possible. Not because someone invents it, but because behavior must reorganize. The absence of form creates pressure. And pressure seeks shape.

As Stalin observed:

“New social ideas grow out of the old social order when the latter is no longer able to solve the problems of society.”

This isn't a theory of progress. It's a description of motion. When the old structure no longer functions, people adapt. They repurpose familiar behaviors. They create new expectations. They test patterns. And over time, those patterns stabilize.

This was visible in the 2024 Hurricane Helene crisis in Western North Carolina. When federal and state institutions failed to respond, community members, many affiliated with the American Communist Party, coordinated relief independently. The slogan “Where is our government?” circulated alongside direct aid. Donations, volunteer work, and mutual defense efforts became expected. Over time, these actions formed a rhythm. A structure. A sub-object.

The government didn't disappear. But its failure to move produced space. The ACP’s motion filled that vacuum. The behavior it organized, donating, sharing, coordinating, became repeatable. And repetition became consequence.

This is how new sub-objects emerge.  
Not from belief. Not from ideology.  
But from motion under pressure.

**Moral Sub-Objects**

If sub-objects organize behavior, then some patterns will be more valuable than others. Not because of who believes in them, but because of what they cause.

A sub-object that reinforces care, mutual defense, or collective memory will produce different consequences than one that enforces silence, hierarchy, or shame. Both are real. Both stabilize behavior. But their moral value isn't in their form, it's in their motion.

This means sub-objects cannot be judged solely by their appearance or their age. Some traditions carry pain across generations. Others preserve insight, comfort, or strength. Their worth depends on how they move people, and what that motion creates.

A structure that compels a person to act against their own judgment may still function as a sub-object. But its function alone does not justify its survival. A system that rewards cruelty or exhaustion may be efficient, but efficiency isn't virtue. Motion, by itself, isn't good. It's only real.

Moral sub-objects are those that preserve or distribute motion without extraction. They allow growth. They make space for contradiction. They remember without calcifying. They guide behavior not by force, but by alignment.

These are not abstract standards. They can be observed. A structure that builds trust will generate resilience. A structure that erodes it will collapse in crisis. A structure that distributes care will replicate. A structure that isolates will break.

Morality isn't a claim. It's a pattern that survives contradiction without becoming coercion.

Memory alone does not guarantee survival. It must stabilize behavior in ways that preserve labor, sustain trust, and organize consequence across generations.

A memory that simply records suffering without organizing resilience does not protect. A memory that preserves tradition without adapting to new contradictions becomes ritual without substance. The value of memory lies not in its preservation alone, but in its ability to direct motion under changing conditions.

This is why morality cannot be treated as a feeling. A good feeling may accompany a moral act, but it's not its cause. The cause is the preservation and extension of structured labor, actions that stabilize life, defend against collapse, and open pathways for growth.

When a moral structure succeeds, it aligns motion with consequence.  
When it fails, it either collapses into sentiment or hardens into coercion.

Labor makes this visible.

After the Great Depression, collective memory of financial collapse stabilized new forms of labor regulation in the United States. Social Security, union protections, and banking reforms were not abstractions. They were structured responses to remembered failure, designed to prevent recurrence. Memory organized labor.

In contrast, post-World War I Germany preserved the memory of betrayal and defeat without successfully reorganizing labor around survival. Resentment grew, but structures to distribute dignity and stability failed. That memory, fragmented, punitive, unstructured, was easily weaponized.

A different pattern appears in religious traditions. Early Christian communities preserved the memory of Christ’s labor, the acts of healing, feeding, and teaching, as central rituals. Where that memory emphasized care and mutual support, it stabilized resilient structures. Where it became ritual divorced from consequence, it hardened into authority.

These examples reveal the stakes.  
A system that teaches care, but cannot organize food, shelter, or defense, has failed materially, even if it feels good.  
A system that organizes labor but extracts it for private gain has succeeded materially, but failed morally.

True moral sub-objects stabilize labor in ways that distribute survival, memory, and motion without exploitation. They don't freeze behavior. They guide it, adapting without losing coherence.

In this sense, morality isn't a dream of goodness. It's a strategy for survival that preserves dignity and prepares for rupture.

**Memory**

And memory, when organized properly, is its first carrier.

Memory, once organized, does not remain fixed. Like all structures built from motion, it evolves, or decays, based on how it's carried.

The repetition of memory stabilizes behavior across generations. But repetition alone does not preserve meaning. Over time, conditions change. New contradictions emerge. Motion shifts. And memory, if unexamined, can become detached from the behaviors it once reinforced.

A ritual originally tied to survival may become symbolic. A warning originally tied to labor may become myth. The structure remains, but the consequence it organized grows faint.

This process can happen slowly, as small distortions accumulate. It can happen violently, through forced revision or ideological capture. And it can happen silently, as memory loses urgency and becomes nostalgia.

The danger isn't forgetting.  
It's remembering without consequence.

A society may remember slogans, anniversaries, or founding myths long after the labor that once sustained them has disappeared. The memory survives in form, but not in function. It ceases to organize behavior. It becomes decoration.

Memory, to remain moral, must be tested against motion. It must not simply exist; it must still organize behavior toward resilience, distribute trust across rupture, and stabilize labor in ways that prepare for future contradiction. A memory that no longer moves action, that no longer builds structures capable of absorbing change, isn't memory in the structural sense. It's fossil, preserved in appearance, but no longer alive.

When memory detaches from labor but retains emotional charge, it becomes vulnerable to capture. A structure that no longer organizes resilience can still organize loyalty, obedience, or fear, if its symbols are kept alive without consequence.

This is corrupted memory. It appears alive because it moves emotion. But it no longer moves labor in ways that preserve survival or dignity. It consumes motion without sustaining it.

Corrupted memory often appears in the form of empty patriotism, ritualized grievance, or weaponized nostalgia. It draws on symbols of past labor, victory, sacrifice, unity, but divorces them from the conditions that gave them meaning.

The result is pressure without purpose. People are moved to act, but not toward survival, not toward trust, not toward dignity. They are moved toward repetition for its own sake, or toward reinforcement of structures that have already decayed.

This isn't harmless. Corrupted memory can organize real labor: conscription, surveillance, scapegoating. It can extract trust without replenishing it. It can produce loyalty without resilience.

The dead structure, if animated by corrupted memory, becomes predatory. It feeds on living motion without offering stability in return.

And because it still carries the form of memory, the flag, the anthem, the slogan, it disguises its deadness. It demands loyalty to a motion that no longer exists.

**Preservation Versus Maintenance**

This is why moral structures must be tested not by their symbols, but by their consequences.  
Not by what they claim to remember.  
But by what they still cause to live.

Moral structures don't maintain themselves. Their survival depends not on belief, but on labor, specifically, the labor of preserving motion across contradiction.

A structure that once preserved trust will decay unless it's revisited, tested, and adjusted. A memory that once stabilized care will fossilize unless it continues to produce new acts of care. A pattern that once carried dignity will become coercion if it's not consciously re-entered.

This is the difference between preservation and maintenance.

Preservation holds form. Maintenance holds function.  
Preservation protects appearance. Maintenance tests consequence.

Living sub-objects require conscious labor. Not because they are fragile, but because they are alive. Their motion must be felt, enacted, and reaffirmed, not just cited or recited.

This labor isn't theoretical. It appears in conversation, correction, repetition, and repair. It appears when a person insists on restoring trust after rupture. When a tradition is adapted to meet present conditions. When a value is defended not through sentiment, but through structure.

This is how we know a moral sub-object is alive: it continues to organize labor without extraction. It produces alignment without obedience. It preserves memory without freezing motion.

And when it fails to do these things, it must be remade, not worshiped.
